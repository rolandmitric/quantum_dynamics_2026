🔸 Interaction Picture of Quantum Mechanics#

We have seen that the transition from the Schrödinger picture to the Heisenberg picture completely removes the time-dependence from the state vector and shifts it to the observables (operators). If \(\left|\psi_s(t)\right\rangle\) is the state vector in the Schrödinger picture, then

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} & \left|\psi_s(t)\right\rangle = U(t) \left|\psi_H(t)\right\rangle \\ & \left|\psi_H(t)\right\rangle = U^{\dagger}(t) \left|\psi_s(t)\right\rangle \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

The expectation values in the Schrödinger picture

\[ \langle A \rangle = \left\langle \psi_s \middle| A_s \middle| \psi_s \right\rangle \]

can be re-expressed as the expectation values in the Heisenberg picture via

\[ \langle A \rangle = \left\langle \psi_s \middle| A_s \middle| \psi_s \right\rangle = \left\langle \psi_H \middle| U^{\dagger} A_s U \middle| \psi_H \right\rangle \]

which motivates the definition of the Heisenberg operator

\[ A_H = U^{\dagger} A U \]

We now introduce a third picture which is particularly useful when the Hamiltonian of the system can be split into two parts, one of which is time-independent:

\[ H = H_0 + V(t) \]

Typically in chemical applications, \(H_0\) would be the molecular or atomic Hamiltonian and \(V(t)\) represents the interaction with some external time-dependent field (e.g. the electric field of a light wave). In such cases, it would be desirable to transform away the “trivial” part of the time-dependence induced by \(H_0\) by moving to a new “rotating” frame of reference. This is the idea of the interaction picture of quantum mechanics, first introduced by Dirac.

From our treatment of the rotating-wave approximation, we recall that we can transform the time evolution operator \(U(t)\) to a new “rotating” system using an arbitrary unitary operator \(R(t)\) such that

\[ U(t) = \left(R R^{\dagger}\right) U(t) = R(t) \tilde{U}(t) \]

This transformation generally modifies the TDSE into an equation of motion for \(\tilde{U}(t)\) as follows:

\[ U(t) = R(t) \tilde{U}(t) \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{dU}{dt} = \frac{dR}{dt} \tilde{U} + R \frac{d\tilde{U}}{dt} \]

Substituting into the TDSE:

\[ i\hbar \frac{dU}{dt} = H U \quad \Rightarrow \quad i\hbar \left( \frac{dR}{dt} \tilde{U} + R \frac{d\tilde{U}}{dt} \right) = H R \tilde{U} \]

From this it follows that:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \frac{d\tilde{U}}{dt} &= R^{\dagger} H R \tilde{U} - i\hbar R^{\dagger} \frac{dR}{dt} \tilde{U} \\ &= H_{\text{eff}}(t) \tilde{U} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

with the effective Hamiltonian:

\[ H_{\text{eff}}(t) = R^{\dagger} H R - i\hbar R^{\dagger} \frac{dR}{dt} \]

We now ask: What is the best choice of \(R(t)\) for a system with a Hamiltonian \(H = H_0 + V(t)\) such that the effective Hamiltonian simplifies optimally?

Let us examine:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} H_{\text{eff}}(t) &= R^{\dagger} \left( H_0 + V \right) R - i\hbar R^{\dagger} \frac{dR}{dt} \\ &= R^{\dagger} H_0 R + R^{\dagger} V R - i\hbar R^{\dagger} \frac{dR}{dt} \\ &= R^{\dagger} \left[ H_0 R - i\hbar \frac{dR}{dt} \right] + R^{\dagger} V R \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

We now see that if we choose \(R(t)\) to satisfy the TDSE for the free Hamiltonian:

\[ i\hbar \frac{dR}{dt} = H_0 R \]

then the first term vanishes, and the effective Hamiltonian becomes:

\[ H_{\text{eff}}(t) = R^{\dagger} V R \]

Thus, choosing \(R\) to be the propagator of the unperturbed system effectively transforms away the time dependence induced by \(H_0\). Since \(H_0\) is time-independent, we can write:

\[ R(t) = e^{-i H_0 t / \hbar} \]

and define the interaction picture propagator \(U_I(t)\) as:

\[ U_I(t) := \tilde{U}(t) = R^{\dagger}(t) U(t) \]

or explicitly:

\[ U_I(t) = e^{i H_0 t / \hbar} U(t) \]

The equation of motion for \(U_I(t)\) becomes:

\[ i\hbar \frac{dU_I}{dt} = R^{\dagger} V R \cdot U_I(t) \]

We define:

\[ V_I(t) := R^{\dagger} V R = e^{i H_0 t / \hbar} V(t) e^{-i H_0 t / \hbar} \]

This is called the interaction picture representation of the operator \(V(t)\). Thus, the equation for the interaction picture propagator reads:

\[ i\hbar \frac{dU_I}{dt} = V_I(t) U_I(t) \]

The intuition is that the transition to the interaction picture removes the rapid oscillations induced by \(H_0\) and leaves only the more slowly varying dynamics caused by \(V(t)\). This separation is meaningful only when the operator norm of \(V(t)\) is small compared to \(H_0\)—a condition that justifies the use of time-dependent perturbation theory.

Time-Dependent Perturbation Theory#

Let us now assume that after transforming to the interaction picture, our problem is reduced to solving:

\[ i\hbar \frac{dU_I}{dt} = V_I(t) U_I(t) \]

This is still a complicated problem, and an exact solution is generally not possible. The idea of perturbation theory is to convert one hard problem into infinitely many simpler ones. We introduce a small formal parameter \(\varepsilon\):

\[ i\hbar \frac{dU_I}{dt} = \varepsilon V_I(t) U_I(t) \]

This makes \(U_I(t)\) now \(\varepsilon\)-dependent:

\[ U_I(t) \to U_I(t, \varepsilon) \]

We assume \(U_I(t, \varepsilon)\) is analytic in \(\varepsilon\), and expand it into a power series:

\[ U_I(t, \varepsilon) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \varepsilon^n U_I^{(n)}(t) \]

Inserting this into the TDSE:

\[ \frac{dU_I}{dt} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \varepsilon^n \frac{dU_I^{(n)}}{dt} \]

We obtain:

\[ i\hbar \sum_{n=0}^\infty \varepsilon^n \frac{dU_I^{(n)}}{dt} = \varepsilon V_I(t) \sum_{n=0}^\infty \varepsilon^n U_I^{(n)}(t) \]

On the right-hand side, we shift the summation index:

\[ \sum_{n=0}^\infty \varepsilon^n \left( i\hbar \frac{dU_I^{(n)}}{dt} \right) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \varepsilon^n V_I(t) U_I^{(n-1)}(t) \]

Matching powers of \(\varepsilon\), we find:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} & i\hbar \frac{dU_I^{(0)}}{dt} = 0 \\ & i\hbar \frac{dU_I^{(n)}}{dt} = V_I(t) U_I^{(n-1)}(t), \quad n = 1, 2, \ldots \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

The zeroth-order equation gives:

\[ U_I^{(0)}(t) = \mathbb{1} \]

Then:

\[ i\hbar \frac{dU_I^{(1)}}{dt} = V_I(t) \]

which integrates to:

\[ U_I^{(1)}(t) = \frac{1}{i\hbar} \int_0^t d\tau_1 \, V_I(\tau_1) \]

Proceeding recursively, we find:

\[ U_I^{(2)}(t) = \frac{1}{(i\hbar)^2} \int_0^t d\tau_2 \int_0^{\tau_2} d\tau_1 \, V_I(\tau_2) V_I(\tau_1) \]

and in general:

\[ U_I^{(n)}(t) = \frac{1}{(i\hbar)^n} \int_0^t d\tau_n \int_0^{\tau_n} d\tau_{n-1} \cdots \int_0^{\tau_2} d\tau_1 \, V_I(\tau_n) \cdots V_I(\tau_1) \]

Thus, the full Dyson series becomes:

\[ U_I(t) = \mathbb{1} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \frac{\varepsilon}{i\hbar} \right)^n \int_{0 < \tau_1 < \cdots < \tau_n < t} V_I(\tau_n) \cdots V_I(\tau_1) \, d\tau_1 \cdots d\tau_n \]

Example: Constant Perturbation — First-Order Time-Dependent Perturbation Theory#

We consider a quantum system described by a time-independent unperturbed Hamiltonian \(H_0\), and a constant (i.e., time-independent) perturbation \(V\). The full Hamiltonian is:

\[ H = H_0 + \varepsilon V \]

Let \( \{ |n\rangle \} \) be the orthonormal eigenbasis of \( H_0 \), such that:

\[ H_0 |n\rangle = E_n |n\rangle \]

Suppose the system is initially in the eigenstate \(|i\rangle\) at time \(t = 0\). We want to compute the probability of finding the system in a different eigenstate \(|f\rangle\) at a later time \(t\), to first order in \( \varepsilon \)).

Step 1: Switch to the Interaction Picture#

The interaction picture time-evolution operator satisfies:

\[ i\hbar \frac{dU_I(t)}{dt} = \varepsilon V_I(t) U_I(t) \]

Since \(V\) is constant and \(H_0\) is time-independent, the interaction-picture perturbation becomes:

\[ V_I(t) = e^{i H_0 t/\hbar} V e^{-i H_0 t/\hbar} \]

This is generally not constant in the interaction picture, even if \(V\) is constant in the Schrödinger picture.

Step 2: First-Order Expansion of the Propagator#

We approximate the interaction-picture evolution operator to first order:

\[ U_I(t) \approx \mathbb{1} - \frac{i \varepsilon}{\hbar} \int_0^t d\tau \, V_I(\tau) \]

Thus, the full time-evolution operator in the Schrödinger picture becomes:

\[ U(t) \approx e^{-i H_0 t / \hbar} \left( \mathbb{1} - \frac{i \varepsilon}{\hbar} \int_0^t d\tau \, V_I(\tau) \right) \]

Step 3: Transition Amplitude#

We compute the transition amplitude from \(|i\rangle\) to \(|f\rangle\):

\[ \langle f | U(t) | i \rangle \approx \langle f | e^{-i H_0 t/\hbar} \left( \mathbb{1} - \frac{i \varepsilon}{\hbar} \int_0^t d\tau \, V_I(\tau) \right) |i\rangle \]

Since \( |i\rangle\) and \(|f\rangle \) are eigenstates of \(H_0\), we have:

\[\begin{split} e^{-i H_0 t/\hbar} |i\rangle = e^{-i E_i t/\hbar} |i\rangle \\ \langle f | e^{-i H_0 t/\hbar} = e^{-i E_f t/\hbar} \langle f | \end{split}\]

Using this, the matrix element becomes:

\[ \langle f | U(t) | i \rangle \approx e^{-i E_f t/\hbar} \left[ \delta_{fi} - \frac{i \varepsilon}{\hbar} \int_0^t d\tau \, \langle f | e^{i H_0 \tau / \hbar} V e^{-i H_0 \tau / \hbar} | i \rangle \right] \]

The matrix element inside the integral becomes:

\[ \langle f | e^{i H_0 \tau / \hbar} V e^{-i H_0 \tau / \hbar} | i \rangle = e^{i (E_f - E_i) \tau / \hbar} \langle f | V | i \rangle \]

Thus:

\[ \langle f | U(t) | i \rangle \approx e^{-i E_f t/\hbar} \left[ \delta_{fi} - \frac{i \varepsilon}{\hbar} \langle f | V | i \rangle \int_0^t d\tau \, e^{i (E_f - E_i) \tau / \hbar} \right] \]

Step 4: Evaluate the Time Integral#

Define the energy difference:

\[ \omega_{fi} := \frac{E_f - E_i}{\hbar} \]

Then:

\[ \int_0^t d\tau \, e^{i \omega_{fi} \tau} = \frac{e^{i \omega_{fi} t} - 1}{i \omega_{fi}} = e^{i \omega_{fi} t/2} \cdot \frac{2 \sin(\omega_{fi} t / 2)}{\omega_{fi}} \]

So:

\[ \langle f | U(t) | i \rangle \approx e^{-i E_f t/\hbar} \left[ \delta_{fi} - \varepsilon \cdot \langle f | V | i \rangle \cdot \frac{e^{i \omega_{fi} t/2} \cdot 2 \sin(\omega_{fi} t / 2)}{\hbar \omega_{fi}} \right] \]

Step 5: Transition Probability#

The transition probability is:

\[ P_{i \rightarrow f}(t) = |\langle f | U(t) | i \rangle|^2 \]

For \( f \neq i \), and to first order in \( \varepsilon\), this becomes:

\[ P_{i \rightarrow f}(t) \approx \left| \varepsilon \cdot \langle f | V | i \rangle \cdot \frac{2 \sin(\omega_{fi} t / 2)}{\hbar \omega_{fi}} \right|^2 \]

This gives the well-known result:

\[ P_{i \rightarrow f}(t) = \frac{4 \varepsilon^2 |\langle f | V | i \rangle|^2}{\hbar^2 \omega_{fi}^2} \cdot \sin^2\left(\frac{\omega_{fi} t}{2}\right) \]

Interpretation#

  • The transition probability oscillates with time.

  • For long times, the function \( \frac{\sin^2(\omega t/2)}{(\omega/2)^2} \) becomes sharply peaked around \(\omega = 0 \), i.e., \( E_f = E_i \).

  • In the limit \( t \to \infty \), this becomes proportional to a delta function, leading to Fermi’s Golden Rule:

\[ \Gamma_{i \rightarrow f} = \frac{2\pi}{\hbar} \varepsilon^2 |\langle f | V | i \rangle|^2 \delta(E_f - E_i) \]

Time-Ordered Form of the Perturbative Expansion#

1. Time Evolution in the Interaction Picture#

Starting from the Dyson series, we now rewrite it by defining the so called the time-ordering operator. This is not that much useful for practical calculations that are of interest for us, but it yields a compact mathematical expression.

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} U_I(t, 0) &= I - \frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t dt_1 \, V_I(t_1) + \left( -\frac{i}{\hbar} \right)^2 \int_0^t dt_2 \int_0^{t_2} dt_1 \, V_I(t_2) V_I(t_1) \\ &\quad + \left( -\frac{i}{\hbar} \right)^3 \int_0^t dt_3 \int_0^{t_3} dt_2 \int_0^{t_2} dt_1 \, V_I(t_3) V_I(t_2) V_I(t_1) + \cdots \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

This expression, written in terms of nested integrals over ordered time variables, becomes:

\[ U_I(t, 0) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \left( -\frac{i}{\hbar} \right)^n \int_{0 \le t_1 \le \cdots \le t_n \le t} dt_n \cdots dt_1 \, V_I(t_n) \cdots V_I(t_1) \]

2. Replacement by Integration over the Cube#

To rewrite the Dyson expansion in a compact form, we define the time-ordering operator \(\mathcal{T}\), which acts on operator products such that the time arguments are rearranged in decreasing order (i.e., later times to the left):

\[ \mathcal{T}[V_I(t_1) \cdots V_I(t_n)] = V_I(t_{\pi(1)}) \cdots V_I(t_{\pi(n)}) \quad \text{with } t_{\pi(1)} \ge \cdots \ge t_{\pi(n)} \]

Then the identity

\[ \int_{0 \le t_1 \le \cdots \le t_n \le t} V_I(t_n) \cdots V_I(t_1) \, dt_n \cdots dt_1 = \frac{1}{n!} \int_{[0, t]^n} \mathcal{T}[V_I(t_1) \cdots V_I(t_n)] \, dt_n \cdots dt_1 \]

allows us to write the Dyson series in compact form:

\[ U_I(t, 0) = \mathcal{T} \exp\left( -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t V_I(t') \, dt' \right) \]

Justification of Time-Ordering Replacement#

Let \(f(t_1, \dots, t_n)\) be an integrable function. Define the simplex:

\[ \Delta = \{ (t_1, \dots, t_n) \in [0,t]^n \mid t_1 \le \cdots \le t_n \} \]

Then:

\[ \int_\Delta f(t_1, \dots, t_n) \, dt_1 \cdots dt_n = \frac{1}{n!} \int_{[0,t]^n} \mathcal{T}[f(t_1, \dots, t_n)] \, dt_1 \cdots dt_n \]

Proof Sketch:#

  1. The cube \([0,t]^n\) can be partitioned into \(n!\) disjoint regions \(D_\pi\), one for each permutation \(\pi \in S_n\), defined by:

\[ D_\pi := \{ (t_1, \dots, t_n) \in [0,t]^n \mid t_{\pi(1)} \le \cdots \le t_{\pi(n)} \} \]

  1. Each point in the cube belongs to exactly one such region, and on each region the function \(f\) can be re-ordered to fit the standard simplex by applying \(\mathcal{T}\).

  2. Therefore:

\[ \int_{[0,t]^n} \mathcal{T}[f(t_1, \dots, t_n)] \, dt_1 \cdots dt_n = \sum_{\pi \in S_n} \int_{D_\pi} f(t_{\pi(1)}, \dots, t_{\pi(n)}) \, dt_1 \cdots dt_n = n! \int_\Delta f(t_1, \dots, t_n) \, dt_1 \cdots dt_n \]

Dividing by \(n!\) gives the result.

Examples#

These identities can be illustrated by working out low-order cases:

For \(n = 2\):

We are integrating over the triangular region where \(0 \le t_1 \le t_2 \le t\), and the operator product \(V(t_2)V(t_1)\) is already time-ordered. The corresponding identity is:

\[ \int_0^t dt_2 \int_0^{t_2} dt_1 \, V(t_2)V(t_1) = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^t dt_1 \int_0^t dt_2 \, \mathcal{T}[V(t_1)V(t_2)] \]

The prefactor \(\frac{1}{2}\) corrects for the fact that the triangular region is half the square \([0,t]^2\), and \(\mathcal{T}\) ensures that the product is always time-ordered regardless of whether \(t_1 > t_2\) or not.

For \(n = 3\):

We integrate over the simplex \(0 \le t_1 \le t_2 \le t_3 \le t\). The product \(V(t_3)V(t_2)V(t_1)\) is time-ordered. The identity becomes:

\[ \int_0^t dt_3 \int_0^{t_3} dt_2 \int_0^{t_2} dt_1 \, V(t_3)V(t_2)V(t_1) = \frac{1}{6} \int_0^t dt_1 dt_2 dt_3 \, \mathcal{T}[V(t_1)V(t_2)V(t_3)] \]

Here, \(\frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{3!}\) accounts for the fact that the simplex occupies \(1/6\) of the cube \([0,t]^3\). Again, the time-ordering operator guarantees the correct operator sequence.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

def plot_dyson_region_n2():
    t = 1
    x = np.linspace(0, t, 100)

    fig, ax = plt.subplots()
    ax.fill_between(x, 0, x, color="lightblue", alpha=0.6, label="t1 ≥ t2")
    ax.plot(x, x, color="blue", linestyle="--")
    ax.set_xlabel("t1")
    ax.set_ylabel("t2")
    ax.set_title("Integration Region for n = 2")
    ax.set_xlim(0, t)
    ax.set_ylim(0, t)
    ax.legend()
    ax.set_aspect("equal")
    plt.grid(True)
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

plot_dyson_region_n2()

import numpy as np
import plotly.graph_objects as go

def plot_dyson_region_n3_interactive():
    # Create 3D grid
    t_vals = np.linspace(0, 1, 50)
    T1, T2 = np.meshgrid(t_vals, t_vals)
    T3 = np.minimum(T1, T2)  # Enforce t1 ≥ t2 ≥ t3

    # Create the surface plot
    fig = go.Figure(data=[
        go.Surface(
            x=T1,
            y=T2,
            z=T3,
            colorscale='jet',
            opacity=0.8,
            showscale=False
        )
    ])

    fig.update_layout(
        title="Integration Region for n = 3: t₁ ≥ t₂ ≥ t₃",
        scene=dict(
            xaxis_title='t₁',
            yaxis_title='t₂',
            zaxis_title='t₃',
            xaxis=dict(range=[0, 1]),
            yaxis=dict(range=[0, 1]),
            zaxis=dict(range=[0, 1]),
        ),
        margin=dict(l=0, r=0, b=0, t=40)
    )

    fig.show()

plot_dyson_region_n3_interactive()
