🔸 Two level system driven by a harmonic field#

We now study the dynamics of a two-level system under a periodic driving potential of the form

\[ V(t) = V_0 \cdot \cos(\omega t), \]

where \(\omega\) is the driving frequency.

The Hamiltonian reads:

\[ H = \frac{1}{2} \Delta \sigma_z + V_0 \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x. \]

In order to make the problem analytically tractable, we introduce an approximation that is commonly known in the literature as the rotating wave approximation (RWA).

The idea is as follows:
By expanding the cosine term,

\[ \cos(\omega t) = \frac{1}{2} \left(e^{i \omega t} + e^{-i \omega t} \right) = \frac{1}{2} e^{i \omega t} \left(1 + e^{-2 i \omega t} \right), \]

the Hamiltonian can be written (in matrix form) as:

\[\begin{split} V(t) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \frac{1}{2} V_0 e^{i \omega t} \left(1 + e^{-2 i \omega t} \right) \\ \frac{1}{2} V_0 e^{-i \omega t} \left(1 + e^{2 i \omega t} \right) & 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{split}\]

Notice that in the second row we have extracted the term \(e^{-i \omega t}\) such that the leading terms are Hermitian conjugates of each other.
We see that the additional terms (\(e^{-2 i \omega t}\) and \(e^{2 i \omega t}\)) rotate with double the frequency and in the opposite direction compared to the \(e^{\pm i \omega t}\) terms.

We will show below that these rapidly oscillating terms can be neglected if the driving frequency is close to resonance and the coupling term is not too large. This will be justified by comparison with the numerically exact solution later.

This is the essence of the rotating wave approximation, often used in the literature:

\[ H = \frac{1}{2} \Delta \sigma_z + V_0 \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x \quad \Rightarrow \quad H_{\text{RWA}} = \frac{1}{2} \Delta \sigma_z + \frac{1}{2} V_0 e^{i \omega t} \sigma_+ + \frac{1}{2} V_0 e^{-i \omega t} \sigma_-. \]

Using the relations:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \sigma_+ &= \sigma_x + i \sigma_y, \\ \sigma_- &= \sigma_x - i \sigma_y, \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

we express the Hamiltonian in terms of the raising and lowering operators.

🔸 Remarks on the Justification of the Rotating Wave Approximation (RWA)#

We now provide two further, more or less heuristic, justifications for the rotating wave approximation (RWA). The first is based on a somewhat overstretched application of the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma from mathematical analysis.

⚬ Mathematical Argument: Riemann–Lebesgue Lemma#

Lemma (Riemann–Lebesgue): Let \(f : \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{C}\) be a function such that \(\int\_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(t)| , dt < \infty\). Then, for any fixed $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$, the following holds:

\[ \lim_{\omega \to \infty} \int_a^b f(t) e^{i \omega t} \, dt = 0. \]

Now, recall that the time evolution operator formally satisfies the differential equation

\[ i \hbar \frac{dU}{dt} = H(t) U(t), \]

which can be written as an integral equation:

\[ U(t) = \mathbb{1} + \frac{1}{i \hbar} \int_0^t H(\tau) U(\tau) \, d\tau. \]

In this form, the fast-oscillating terms in the Hamiltonian—such as those proportional to $e^{2i \omega t}$—will lead to integrals of the type:

\[ \int_0^t e^{2i \omega \tau} U(\tau) \, d\tau, \]

while slower-oscillating terms (like $e^{i \omega t}$) contribute as:

\[ \int_0^t e^{i \omega \tau} U(\tau) \, d\tau. \]

By invoking the Riemann–Lebesgue lemma, we expect that integrals involving rapidly oscillating factors (i.e., higher powers of \(\omega\)) will become small in the limit \(\omega \to \infty\). Therefore, the RWA is based on the assumption that terms oscillating with \(2\omega\) can be neglected, as if \(2\omega\) were “effectively infinite.”

⚬ Physical Argument: Energy Conservation and Photon Processes#

A more physical and intuitive justification comes from analyzing the interaction term in the Hamiltonian. Suppose we couple a two-level system via a time-dependent term of the form:

\[ V(t) = V_0 \cos(\omega t) \cdot \sigma_x. \]

Using the ladder operators defined as

\[ \sigma_+ = \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_x + i \sigma_y), \quad \sigma_- = \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_x - i \sigma_y), \]

we can rewrite the coupling term:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} V(t) &= V_0 \cos(\omega t)(\sigma_+ + \sigma_-) \\ &= \frac{V_0}{2} \left(e^{i \omega t} + e^{-i \omega t} \right)(\sigma_+ + \sigma_-) \\ &= \frac{V_0}{2} \left( e^{i \omega t} \sigma_+ + e^{i \omega t} \sigma_- + e^{-i \omega t} \sigma_+ + e^{-i \omega t} \sigma_- \right). \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

We can now interpret the exponential terms as follows:

  • $e^{i \omega t} \sigma_+$: Photon absorption with system excitation.

  • $e^{-i \omega t} \sigma_-$: Photon emission with system de-excitation.

These are the energy-conserving terms and are retained in the RWA.

However, the other two terms:

  • $e^{i \omega t} \sigma_-$: System de-excites while absorbing a photon.

  • $e^{-i \omega t} \sigma_+$: System excites while emitting a photon.

These describe processes that violate energy conservation in the rotating frame and are typically referred to as non-resonant or virtual terms. Intuitively, we expect these to be negligible because they represent highly off-resonant processes. This provides a physically motivated basis for neglecting them in the RWA.

🔸 Derivation of the time-evolution operator in the RWA approximation#

The Hamiltonian of the system in the RWA approximation limit may be now written in the form:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} H_{\text{RWA}} &= \frac{1}{2} \Delta \sigma_z + \frac{1}{2} V_0 e^{i \omega t} (\sigma_x + i \sigma_y) + \frac{1}{2} V_0 e^{-i \omega t} (\sigma_x - i \sigma_y) \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \Delta \sigma_z + V_0 \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x - V_0 \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_y \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

We now proceed to calculate the time-evolution operator in the RWA approximation:

\[ \frac{dU}{dt} = H_{\text{RWA}} U = \left[ \frac{1}{2} \Delta \sigma_z + V_0 \left( \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x - \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_y \right) \right] U \]

To simplify the solution, we move to a rotating frame of reference.
The intuition behind this is that in the rotating frame the coupling term appears static, reducing the problem to a previously solved case of a constant perturbation.
Thus, we define:

\[ U(t) = e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \, \tilde{U}(t) \]

The time derivative of \(U\) becomes:

\[ \frac{dU}{dt} = -\frac{i}{2} \omega \sigma_z U + e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \frac{d\tilde{U}}{dt} \]

Inserting into the time-dependent Schrödinger equation for \(U(t)\) gives:

\[ \frac{\hbar \omega}{2} \sigma_z e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \tilde{U} + i \hbar e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \frac{d\tilde{U}}{dt} = \frac{1}{2} \Delta \sigma_z e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \tilde{U} + \left( V_0 \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x - V_0 \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_y \right) e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \tilde{U} \]

By defining \(\Delta = \hbar \omega_0\), we can write:

\[ i \hbar e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \frac{d\tilde{U}}{dt} = \frac{1}{2} \hbar (\omega_0 - \omega) \sigma_z e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \tilde{U} + \left( V_0 \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x - V_0 \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_y \right) e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \tilde{U} \]

We can now multiply both sides by \(e^{\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z}\) from the left, getting:

We now have:

\[ \frac{d\tilde{U}}{dt} = \left[ \frac{1}{2} \hbar(\omega_0 - \omega) \sigma_z + V_0 \, e^{\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \left( \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x - \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_y \right) e^{-\frac{i}{2} \omega t \sigma_z} \right] \tilde{U} \]

To simplify this, we need to compute the similarity transformations of the Pauli matrices under rotations generated by \(\sigma_z\). Specifically:

Rotation of Pauli matrices#

We use the identity

\[ e^{i \theta \sigma_z} \, \sigma_x \, e^{-i \theta \sigma_z} = \cos(2\theta) \, \sigma_x + \sin(2\theta) \, \sigma_y, \]

and similarly,

\[ e^{i \theta \sigma_z} \, \sigma_y \, e^{-i \theta \sigma_z} = \cos(2\theta) \, \sigma_y - \sin(2\theta) \, \sigma_x. \]

These follow from the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formula or by applying the Euler operator identity:

\[ e^{i A x} = \cos(x) \cdot I + i A \sin(x), \]

which is valid when \(A^2 = I\). For Pauli matrices, \(\sigma_z^2 = I\).

Apply to the time-dependent rotation#

Set \(\theta = \frac{\omega t}{2}\). Then:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} e^{i \frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z} \sigma_x e^{-i \frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z} &= \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x + \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_y, \\ e^{i \frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z} \sigma_y e^{-i \frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z} &= \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_y - \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_x. \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Therefore, the rotated expression becomes:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} & e^{i \frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z} \left( \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x - \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_y \right) e^{-i \frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z} \\ &= \cos(\omega t) \left[ \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x + \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_y \right] - \sin(\omega t) \left[ \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_y - \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_x \right] \\ &= \left( \cos^2(\omega t) + \sin^2(\omega t) \right) \sigma_x + 2 \cos(\omega t) \sin(\omega t) \sigma_y - 2 \cos(\omega t) \sin(\omega t) \sigma_y \\ &= \sigma_x. \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Hence, the entire rotated term simplifies beautifully to:

\[ e^{i \frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z} \left( \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x - \sin(\omega t) \, \sigma_y \right) e^{-i \frac{\omega t}{2} \sigma_z} = \sigma_x. \]

Final simplified equation#

The time-evolution equation in the rotating frame now reads:

\[ \frac{d\tilde{U}}{dt} = \left[ \frac{1}{2} \hbar(\omega_0 - \omega) \sigma_z + V_0 \sigma_x \right] \tilde{U}. \]

This is a time-independent Schrödinger equation in the rotating frame.
It describes a two-level system under a static effective Hamiltonian:

\[ H_{\text{eff}} = \frac{1}{2} \hbar(\omega_0 - \omega) \sigma_z + V_0 \sigma_x. \]

Solution Using Time-Evolution Operator#

In order to propagate the system in the rotating frame we can now use the time-evolution operator that we constructed for a non-resonant coupling case. We get for \(U(t)\):

\[ U(t) = \cos\left(\frac{\Omega_R t}{2}\right) \, \mathbb{I} - i \sin\left(\frac{\Omega_R t}{2}\right) \left( \frac{2V_0}{\hbar \Omega_R} \sigma_x + \frac{\delta}{\Omega_R} \sigma_z \right). \]

Assume the initial state is the ground state:

\[\begin{split} |\psi(0)\rangle = |0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 0\end{pmatrix}. \end{split}\]

Then the evolved state is: $\( |\psi(t)\rangle = U(t) |\psi(0)\rangle. \)$

Carrying out the multiplication: $$ |\psi(t)\rangle = \left[ \cos\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right)

  • i \frac{\delta}{\Omega_R} \sin\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right) \right] \begin{pmatrix}1 \ 0\end{pmatrix}

  • i \frac{2V_0}{\hbar \Omega_R} \sin\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right) \begin{pmatrix}0 \ 1\end{pmatrix}. $$

So the wavefunction becomes: $$ |\psi(t)\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right)

  • i \frac{\delta}{\Omega_R} \sin\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right) \

  • i \frac{2V_0}{\hbar \Omega_R} \sin\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right) \end{pmatrix}. $$

From this, the population in the excited state is:

\[ P_{\text{excited}}(t) = \left| - i \frac{2V_0}{\hbar \Omega_R} \sin\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right) \right|^2 = \frac{4V_0^2}{\hbar^2 \Omega_R^2} \sin^2\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right). \]

And since \(\Omega_R^2 = \delta^2 + \left( \frac{2V_0}{\hbar} \right)^2\), we recover the familiar expression:

\[ P_{\text{excited}}(t) = \frac{\left(\frac{2V_0}{\hbar}\right)^2} {\delta^2 + \left(\frac{2V_0}{\hbar}\right)^2} \cdot \sin^2\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right). \]

This completes the derivation using the general time-evolution operator for a two-level system with constant coupling.

The MultiLevel Class Implementation#

We implement a simple Python class to define and propagate quantum states in an N-level system.

import numpy as np
from scipy.linalg import expm
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

class MultiLevel:
    def __init__(self, ndim=2):
        self.ndim = ndim
        self.h = np.zeros((ndim, ndim), dtype=complex)  
        self.V = lambda t: np.zeros((ndim, ndim), dtype=complex)  
        
    def set_hamiltonian(self, diagonal):
        np.fill_diagonal(self.h, diagonal)

    def set_coupling(self, V_func):
        self.V = V_func

    def getH(self, t):
        return self.h + self.V(t)

    def propagate(self, psi0, dt, nstep):

        psi0 = np.asarray(psi0, dtype=complex)
        psi_t = np.zeros((nstep + 1, self.ndim), dtype=complex)
        psi_t[0] = psi0

        for i in range(1, nstep + 1):
            t = (i - 1) * dt + dt/2
            H = self.getH(t)
            U = expm(-1j * H * dt)
            psi_t[i] = U @ psi_t[i - 1]

        return psi_t

Numerical vs Analytical (RWA) Propagation of a Two-Level System#

We now use the MultiLevel class to simulate a driven two-level system and compare it to the analytical solution obtained using the Rotating Wave Approximation (RWA). This allows us to validate the RWA in the weak-driving, near-resonant regime, and to observe its breakdown outside this regime.

We consider a Hamiltonian of the form:

\[ H(t) = \frac{1}{2} \hbar \Delta \sigma_z + V_0 \cos(\omega t) \, \sigma_x \]

and compare the numerical solution to the analytical RWA result:

\[ P_{\text{exc}}^{\text{RWA}}(t) = \frac{4 V_0^2}{\hbar^2 \Omega_R^2} \sin^2\left( \frac{\Omega_R t}{2} \right), \quad \text{with } \Omega_R = \sqrt{\delta^2 + \left(\frac{2 V_0}{\hbar}\right)^2},\; \delta = \omega_0 - \omega. \]

Two-Level System with harmonic coupling: Numerical propagation#

Consider a two-level system with energies \( E_0 = 0 \), \( E_1 = \Delta \), and a Gaussian coupling:

\[ V_{01} = V_{10} = V_0 \cos(\omega t) \]
Delta = 1.0     # Energy difference
V0 = 0.1       # Coupling strength
omega = 1.0    # Frequency of a light-field
coupling = lambda t: np.array([[0, V0*np.cos(omega*t) ],
                               [V0 *np.cos(omega*t) , 0]], dtype=complex)

sys = MultiLevel(ndim=2)
sys.set_hamiltonian([0, Delta])
sys.set_coupling(coupling)
psi0=np.array([1.0,0.0],dtype = complex)

nstep = 1000
dt = 0.1
psi_t = sys.propagate(psi0, dt, nstep)

pop = np.abs(psi_t)**2
coh = np.einsum('ti,tj->tij',psi_t.conj(),psi_t)
time = np.linspace(0,100.0,1000+1)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(time, pop[:,0], lw = 3.0, color = "red")
plt.plot(time, pop[:,1], lw = 3.0, color = "blue")
plt.plot(time, coh[:,0,1], "--")
plt.xlabel('Time')
plt.ylabel('Population')
plt.title('Two-Level System Driven by harmonic coupling')
plt.grid(True)

Comparison between the RWA and numerical propagation for a resonant coupling#

# Parameters
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.linalg import expm

Δ = 1.0       # Energy splitting
V = .3      # Driving strength
ω = 1.0     # Driving frequency (resonant)
dt = 0.01
t_max = 1000
times = np.arange(0, t_max, dt)

# Initial state: ground state |g> = [1, 0]
ψ = np.array([1.0, 0.0], dtype=complex)
ψs = []

# Time evolution under full Hamiltonian
for t in times:
    H = (Δ/2)*np.array([[1,0],[0,-1]]) + V*np.cos(ω*(t+dt/2))*np.array([[0,1],[1,0]])
    U = expm(-1j * H * dt)
    ψ = U @ ψ
    ψs.append(np.abs(ψ[1])**2)  # Excited state population

P_rwa = np.sin(0.5 * V * times)**2


# Plot comparison
plt.plot(times, ψs, color = "red", label='Numerical', lw=2)
plt.plot(times, P_rwa, color = "blue", label='RWA', lw=2)
plt.xlabel('Time')
plt.ylabel('Excited State Population')
plt.title('Numerical vs RWA')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.xlim(900,1000)
plt.show()
